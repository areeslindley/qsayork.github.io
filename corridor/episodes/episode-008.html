Key Concepts

TipLast Stone First End (LSFE)

The team that throws the final stone in the first end gains a significant strategic advantage, determined by winning the “draw shot challenge” (getting closest to the centre). Analysis of the Olympic data shows that over 60% of matches were won by the team with last stone first end, though this may partly reflect that better teams tend to win the challenge.

TipBlanking the End

A strategic decision where a team deliberately ensures no stones remain in the house so that nobody scores, allowing them to retain the hammer (last stone) for the next end. This tactic reflects the trade-off between scoring immediately and preserving positional advantage for later, more crucial ends.

TipThe Hammer in the Final End

Conventional curling wisdom suggests you want the last stone in the final end to control the outcome. However, analysis shows only about 40% of matches are won by the team with the hammer in the final end—partly because by that point, the match outcome may already be decided.

Discussion

This episode showcases how sports analytics can both illuminate and complicate our understanding of match outcomes. The analysis of last stone advantage demonstrates a classic problem in sports statistics: separating the effect of a tactical variable from the confounding influence of team quality. Better teams win the draw shot challenge and win more matches, but is it the challenge victory or their superior skill that drives success?

The discussion also touches on the ethics of sports analytics, particularly whether statistical evidence can detect rule-breaking. This connects to broader debates in sports about fair play, the role of officials, and whether “gaming the rules” should be distinguished from outright cheating. Curling’s self-policing tradition makes it a fascinating case study for how different sports cultures approach integrity and competition.
